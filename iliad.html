The Iliad

Chronicle of the Wrath of Achilles

"Sing, O goddess, the anger of Achilles son of Peleus,
that brought countless ills upon the Achaeans."

Homer's Iliad unfolds across 51 days in the tenth year of the Trojan War, driven by a single act of pride and the devastating chain of wrath it unleashes. This chronicle traces every major turning point — from Apollo's plague to Hector's burial.

Act I · The Wrath Begins
Book I · Day 1–12

Apollo's Plague

The priest Chryses begs Agamemnon to return his daughter. Refused, he prays to Apollo, who rains arrows of pestilence upon the Greek camp for nine days.

Apollo Agamemnon Chryses
"Far-shooting Apollo" descends from Olympus, his arrows clattering on his silver bow. For nine days the pyres burn. On the tenth, Achilles calls an assembly — the first crack in the Greek command structure that will fracture into open war between heroes.
Book I · Day 10

The Quarrel of Kings

Achilles demands Agamemnon return Chryseis. Agamemnon agrees — then seizes Achilles' prize, the captive Briseis. Achilles erupts in fury.

Achilles Agamemnon Briseis
Achilles draws his sword — then Athena appears, invisible to all others, and restrains him. He sheathes it, hurling words instead. "You wine-sack with the eyes of a dog." He vows never to fight for the Greeks again, a vow that will cost thousands their lives.
Book I · Day 10–21

Achilles Withdraws

Achilles retreats to his ships and prays to his mother Thetis. She ascends to Olympus and asks Zeus to let the Trojans prevail until her son is honored.

Achilles Thetis Zeus
Zeus nods — the divine gesture that shakes Olympus — and the fate of thousands is sealed. This "plan of Zeus" runs throughout the epic as the gods manipulate the war to fulfill this single promise. Hera immediately suspects and quarrels with Zeus; Hephaestus makes peace on Olympus while below, men will die.
Act II · The War Without Achilles
Book II · Day 22

The Catalogue of Ships

Zeus sends a false dream urging Agamemnon to attack. The Greek host musters — Homer lists every contingent in the famous Catalogue, 1,186 ships from 29 kingdoms.

Agamemnon Zeus Odysseus
Thersites, the ugliest and most insolent of the Greeks, urges the army to sail home. Odysseus beats him into silence. The Catalogue of Ships that follows is among the most debated passages in ancient literature — possibly drawn from a Bronze Age administrative source.
Book III · Day 22

Paris vs. Menelaus

To spare further bloodshed, Paris challenges Menelaus to single combat. Whoever wins keeps Helen and ends the war. Menelaus wins — but Aphrodite spirits Paris away.

Paris Menelaus Aphrodite Helen
Menelaus seizes Paris by the helm and drags him across the field. The strap breaks. Aphrodite wraps Paris in a mist and deposits him in his bedchamber with Helen, who scorns him — briefly. The duel that should have ended everything instead changes nothing. The war must go on.
Book IV · Day 22

The Truce Broken

Athena tricks the Trojan archer Pandarus into shooting Menelaus, shattering the truce. Full battle erupts across the plain of Troy.

Athena Pandarus Menelaus
The arrow pierces Menelaus's belt — a wound, not fatal, deliberately so by Athena's hand. The gods favor different sides; neither will let the war end peacefully. Agamemnon rallies the troops with a speech mixing encouragement and insult in equal measure.
Books V–VI · Day 22

Diomedes' Aristeia

Diomedes rampages through the Trojan host, wounding Aphrodite and even Ares himself. He stands as the epic's greatest fighter while Achilles sits idle.

Diomedes Aphrodite Ares Athena
An aristeia is a hero's moment of supreme battlefield glory. Diomedes' is the longest in the poem. Athena guides his spear-thrust into Ares's flank — the god of war himself cries out "as loud as nine or ten thousand men" and flees to Olympus to complain. Zeus mocks him.
Book VI · Day 22

Hector & Andromache

Hector enters Troy to arrange prayers. He finds his wife Andromache and infant son Astyanax at the city walls — their farewell, one of the most tender scenes in all of literature.

Hector Andromache Astyanax
Hector reaches for his son, but the child cries at his father's gleaming helmet. Hector removes it, laughing, and holds the boy aloft in prayer. Andromache weeps, foreseeing his death. He knows he will die, that Troy will fall — and goes back to battle anyway. This is heroism: not hope, but duty.
Book VII · Day 23

Ajax vs. Hector

Hector challenges any Greek to single combat. Ajax the Greater answers. They fight to a standstill at sunset; heralds separate them and they exchange gifts.

Ajax Hector
The duel ends in mutual respect — a rare pause in the poem's brutality. Ajax gives Hector his sword-belt; Hector gives Ajax his purple sword. Both gifts will later become instruments of tragedy: Ajax will use Hector's sword in his own suicide, and Achilles will drag Hector's body by the belt.
Books IX–X · Day 24

The Embassy to Achilles

With the Greeks badly pressed, Agamemnon sends Phoenix, Ajax, and Odysseus to offer Achilles vast gifts and Briseis returned. Achilles refuses all.

Achilles Odysseus Ajax Phoenix
Achilles' speech to the embassy is the most philosophically dense in the poem. He rejects the heroic code itself: "A man's life cannot come back again, it cannot be lifted or captured." He speaks of two fates — long life without glory, or a short life immortalized in song. He has not yet decided.
Act III · The Fall of Patroclus
Books XIII–XV · Days 25–26

The Trojans Breach the Wall

While Zeus is distracted by Hera's seduction, Poseidon bolsters the Greeks. When Zeus wakes, he drives the Trojans to the Greek ships — Hector breaches the wall and sets ships ablaze.

Hector Zeus Hera Poseidon
Hera seduces Zeus on Mount Ida, borrowing Aphrodite's girdle of desire. While Zeus sleeps, Poseidon rallies the Greeks. Zeus wakes furious, accuses Hera, then reasserts control with devastating swiftness — reminding gods and men alike of his supreme power.
Book XVI · Day 27

Patroclus Enters the Field

Unable to bear watching the Greeks burn, Patroclus begs Achilles to let him lead the Myrmidons in Achilles' armor. Achilles agrees — but warns him not to pursue the Trojans to the walls.

Patroclus Achilles
Patroclus weeps as he pleads with Achilles — a remarkable image of the era's most feared warrior being moved by his companion's tears. Achilles' warning is clear: rout them, save the ships, come back. Pride and glory drive Patroclus beyond the limit. He will not come back.
Book XVI · Day 27

The Death of Patroclus

Patroclus routs the Trojans and reaches the city walls — ignoring Achilles' command. Apollo stuns him; Euphorbus wounds him; Hector delivers the killing blow.

Patroclus Hector Apollo Euphorbus
Apollo strips Patroclus of Achilles' divine armor. Dazed, he stands weaponless. Euphorbus spears him from behind. Hector finishes him. His last words to Hector are a prophecy: "You yourself are not long for the light — already death and powerful fate are standing beside you." The wrath of Achilles enters its final, terrible phase.
Act IV · The Return of Achilles
Books XVII–XVIII · Day 27

Achilles Learns & Grieves

Antilochus brings word to Achilles. He collapses in the dust, screaming. His grief is so terrible that Thetis hears him from beneath the sea and ascends to mourn with him.

Achilles Thetis Antilochus
The sound of Achilles' grief makes the sea-nymphs weep and all the Nereids gather. Thetis holds her son and asks what has happened. He tells her. She weeps — knowing what his return to battle means. His fate: if he avenges Patroclus, he will die soon after. He chooses it without hesitation.
Book XVIII · Night of Day 27

The Shield of Achilles

Thetis commissions Hephaestus to forge new divine armor. On the great shield, the god hammers the entire world — cities at peace and war, harvests, dances, the ocean's rim.

Hephaestus Thetis
The Shield ekphrasis (a literary description of an artwork) is Homer's image of the world outside the war — life continuing, beautiful and brutal in equal measure, indifferent to the heroes' fates. It places the Iliad's violence inside the context of all human experience. The shield is the world; Achilles carries the world into battle.
Books XIX–XXI · Day 28

Achilles' Aristeia

Achilles reconciles with Agamemnon, dons his new armor, and re-enters battle. He cuts through the Trojan host like a wildfire, filling the Scamander river with corpses.

Achilles Agamemnon Xanthus (river god)
The river Xanthus rises against Achilles, choked with the dead he has piled in it. Achilles nearly drowns — the one moment the invincible warrior is in mortal peril from the land itself. Hephaestus and Poseidon intervene. Even the gods must work to contain him.
Act V · Hector's End & Reconciliation
Book XXII · Day 28

The Death of Hector

Hector stands alone outside the gates as all of Troy watches. He flees. Achilles chases him three times around the city walls before Athena tricks Hector into turning to fight.

Hector Achilles Athena Zeus
Zeus lifts his golden scales — Hector's fate sinks. Athena appears to Hector as his brother Deiphobos, urging him to stand and fight together. He turns, hurls his spear, reaches for his second — finds no one. Understands the trick. "The gods have summoned me to death." He draws his sword and charges. Achilles' spear finds the gap between neck and shoulder.
Book XXII · Day 28

The Desecration of Hector

Achilles pierces Hector's heels, lashes him behind his chariot, and drags him around Patroclus's tomb. He repeats this for twelve days. The gods look on, disturbed.

Achilles Hector Apollo
Apollo shields the body from decay and disfigurement. Even in death, the gods protect what remains of Hector's dignity. Meanwhile on Olympus, Zeus and Apollo debate whether to let the desecration continue. It has crossed a line — even by this world's brutal standards, Achilles has gone too far.
Book XXIII · Days 29–40

Funeral Games for Patroclus

Achilles holds elaborate funeral games in Patroclus's honor — chariot races, boxing, wrestling, archery. He presides with unexpected generosity.

Achilles Diomedes Ajax Odysseus
The games are a small world of Greek values — competition, excellence, honor — compressed into a day. Achilles, transformed by grief, settles disputes calmly, grants prizes generously, even gives old Nestor a consolation gift. The man who could not share his prize-girl now gives freely. Grief has taught him something about what matters.
Book XXIV · Day 51

Priam Ransoms Hector

Zeus commands Achilles to accept ransom. Guided by Hermes through the Greek camp, the aged king Priam enters Achilles' tent alone and begs for his son's body — invoking the name of Achilles' own father.

Priam Achilles Zeus Hermes
"I have endured what no man has endured before — I have kissed the hands of the man who killed my children." Achilles weeps — for Patroclus, for his own father Peleus whom he will never see again. They weep together, enemy and conqueror, in one of the most moving moments in world literature. He agrees. He gives back the body.
Book XXIV · Day 51

Hector's Burial

Troy mourns for nine days. On the tenth, Hector is placed on the pyre. The Iliad ends here — not with the fall of Troy or the death of Achilles, but with the burial of Troy's greatest defender.

Hector Andromache Priam Hecuba
"So the Trojans buried Hector, breaker of horses." The final line is deliberately understated. Troy will fall, Achilles will die, Priam will be slaughtered — but Homer does not show us these things. The Iliad is not a story of victory or defeat. It is a meditation on wrath, loss, and the brief flicker of human life against the indifference of the gods.

Homer · 8th Century BCE · Translated by numerous hands across millennia
